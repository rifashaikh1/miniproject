AWARENESS OF CANCER

What is Cancer?


Cancer is a group of diseases characterized by the uncontrolled growth and spread of abnormal cells in the body. These cells can form tumors or invade surrounding tissues and organs. It can occur in almost any part of the body and may spread to other areas through the bloodstream or lymphatic system. Early detection and treatment are crucial for managing cancer and improving outcomes.

1. Bladder Cancer
Overview: Affects the bladder, the organ that stores urine.Bladder cancer occurs when malignant (cancerous) cells form in the tissues of the bladder, a hollow organ in the lower abdomen that stores urine. It is one of the most common cancers, especially in older adults, and occurs more frequently in men than women.
Symptoms: -
  • Blood in urine (hematuria)*: The most common symptom, which may appear bright red or cola-colored.
  • Frequent urination*: A persistent need to urinate.
  • Painful urination*: Discomfort or burning sensation while urinating.
  • Lower back pain*: Especially if it is localized on one side.

Risk Factors:
  • Blood in urine (hematuria): The most common symptom, which may appear bright red or cola-colored.
  • Frequent urination: A persistent need to urinate.
  • Painful urination: Discomfort or burning sensation while urinating.
  • Lower back pain*: Especially if it is localized on one side.

Treatment:
  • Surgery
  • chemotherapy
  • radiation
  • immunotherapy
2. Breast Cancer
Overview: Forms in the breast cells, primarily affecting women but can occur in men. The disease occurs when cells in the breast begin to grow uncontrollably, forming a tumor that can be felt as a lump or seen on imaging tests. Early detection and treatment significantly improve outcomes.
Symptoms:
  • Lump in the breast: The most common symptom is a new lump or mass in the breast that feels hard or has irregular edges.
  • - Changes in breast size or shape*.
  • - Dimpling of the skin: A puckering or dimpling of the skin, resembling an orange peel.
  • - Nipple discharge: Unexplained fluid or blood from the nipple.
  • - Inverted nipple: A sudden change where the nipple turns inward.
  • - Breast or nipple pain.
  • - Swelling or thickening: Especially in part of the breast or underarm area.
  • - Redness or flaky skin: Especially on the nipple or breast.

Risk Factors:
  • Gender: Women are at a much higher risk, but men can develop breast cancer too.
  • Age: Risk increases with age, especially after age 50.
  • Family history: Having close relatives with breast cancer increases the risk, especially if they were diagnosed at a young age.
  • Genetic mutations: Mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes significantly raise the risk of breast and ovarian cancers.
  • Hormonal factors: Early menstruation (before age 12), late menopause (after age 55), hormone replacement therapy, and exposure to estrogen over a long period can increase risk.
  • Lifestyle factors: Obesity, alcohol consumption, and lack of physical activity can also raise the risk.
  • Radiation exposure: Women who had radiation therapy to the chest before age 30 have a higher risk.

Treatment:
  • Surgery (mastectomy or lumpectomy)
  • radiation
  • chemotherapy
  • hormonal therapy.
3. Colorectal Cancer
Overview: Affects the colon or rectum, part of the digestive tract.Colorectal cancer (CRC) refers to cancer that starts in the colon (large intestine) or the rectum. It is one of the most common types of cancer worldwide but is highly preventable and treatable if detected early. Colorectal cancer usually begins as small, non-cancerous (benign) clumps of cells called polyps that form on the inner lining of the colon or rectum. Over time, some of these polyps may become cancerous. It is one of the most common types of cancer worldwide but is highly preventable and treatable if detected early. Colorectal cancer usually begins as small, non-cancerous (benign) clumps of cells called polyps that form on the inner lining of the colon or rectum. Over time, some of these polyps may become cancerous.
Symptoms:
  • Changes in bowel habits: Diarrhea, constipation, or narrowing of the stool that lasts for more than a few days.
  • Rectal bleeding or blood in the stool (which may make it look dark or tar-like).
  • Abdominal discomfort: Cramping, pain, or bloating.
  • unnexplained weight loss.
  • Weakness or fatigue.
  • Feeling like the bowel doesn’t completely empty* after a bowel movement.

Risk Factors:
  • Age: The risk increases significantly after age 50, although cases are rising in younger adults.
  • Family history: A personal or family history of colorectal cancer or polyps can raise the risk.
  • Genetic mutations: Conditions like familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) and Lynch syndrome increase risk due to inherited gene mutations.
  • Diet: Diets high in red meat, processed meats, and low in fiber can increase the risk.
  • Lifestyle factors: Lack of physical activity, obesity, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption are associated with higher risk.
  • Chronic conditions: Inflammatory bowel diseases, such as Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis, increase the likelihood of colorectal cancer

Treatment:
  • Surgery
  • chemotherapy
  • radiation
  • targeted therapy

4. Kidney (Renal Cell) Cancer
Overview: Develops in the kidneys, which filter waste from the blood.It begins in the lining of the small tubes in the kidney, which are responsible for filtering blood and producing urine. While RCC is the most prevalent type of kidney cancer, there are other less common forms as well.
Symptoms:
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria)*: It may be visible or detected through tests.
  • Lower back pain*: Typically on one side, and not due to injury.
  • A lump or mass*: That can be felt in the side or lower back.
  • Unexplained weight loss*.
  • Fatigue.
  • Fever*: That comes and goes without infection.
  • High blood pressure or elevated calcium levels*: Occasionally, these are detected during routine tests.

Risk Factors:
  • Smoking*: Increases the risk significantly, as harmful chemicals can damage kidney cells over time.
  • Age*: Most kidney cancers are diagnosed in people over 60.
  • Gender*: Men are twice as likely as women to develop kidney cancer.
  • Obesity*: Excess weight increases the risk by causing hormonal imbalances.
  • High blood pressure*: Hypertension is linked to an increased risk of kidney cancer.
  • Family history*: A family history of kidney cancer, especially in first-degree relatives, raises the risk.
  • Inherited genetic conditions*: Conditions like von Hippel-Lindau disease, hereditary papillary RCC, and Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome increase the risk of kidney cancer.
  • Chronic kidney disease or dialysis*: People on long-term dialysis have a higher chance of developing RCC.
  • Occupational exposure*: Exposure to certain chemicals, such as asbestos, cadmium, and trichloroethylene, is associated with an increased risk.

Treatment:
  • Surgery
  • targeted therapy,
  • immunotherapy
5. Lung Cancer
Overview: Affects the lungs, often caused by smoking or exposure to toxins.It is one of the leading causes of cancer-related deaths globally, primarily caused by smoking but can also affect non-smokers. Lung cancer occurs when cells in the lungs grow uncontrollably, forming tumors that interfere with the lung's function and may spread to other parts of the body.
Symptoms:
  • Persistent cough*: That doesn’t go away or gets worse.
  • Coughing up blood* (hemoptysis).
  • Chest pain*: That is worse with deep breathing, coughing, or laughing.
  • Shortness of breath*.
  • Hoarseness*.
  • Unexplained weight loss*.
  • Fatigue*.
  • Recurring respiratory infections*: Such as bronchitis or pneumonia.
  • Wheezing*.

Risk Factors:
  • Smoking*: The leading cause of lung cancer. The risk increases with the number of cigarettes smoked and the duration of smoking.
  • Secondhand smoke*: Exposure to cigarette smoke from others can increase the risk of lung cancer.
  • Radon exposure*: A naturally occurring radioactive gas that can accumulate in homes and increase lung cancer risk.
  • Asbestos exposure*: A substance used in construction and manufacturing that can cause lung cancer, particularly in those who smoke.
  • Air pollution*: Long-term exposure to polluted air can increase the risk, especially in urban areas.
  • Family history*: A family history of lung cancer can increase risk.

Treatment:
  • Surgery
  • targeted therapy,
  • immunotherapy
  • Chemotherapy
6. Lymphoma
Overview:Lymphoma is a type of cancer that begins in the lymphatic system, a vital part of the immune system, which includes the lymph nodes, spleen, thymus, and bone marrow. Lymphoma occurs when lymphocytes, a type of white blood cell, grow uncontrollably and form tumors in the lymph nodes or other parts of the lymphatic system. It is one of the most common blood cancers.
Symptoms: - *Swollen lymph nodes*: Painless swelling of lymph nodes in the neck, armpits, or groin. - *Fever*: Persistent or recurring without an infection. - *Night sweats*. - *Unexplained weight loss*. - *Fatigue*. - *Shortness of breath* or cough: Due to swollen lymph nodes pressing on the lungs. - *Itchy skin*. - *Abdominal pain or swelling*: If the lymphoma affects lymph nodes in the abdomen. .
Risk Factors:1. *Weakened immune system*: People with autoimmune diseases or those taking immunosuppressive drugs after organ transplants are at higher risk. 2. *Infections*: Certain viral and bacterial infections are linked to lymphoma, such as Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), HIV, and Helicobacter pylori. 3. *Age*: Hodgkin lymphoma is more common in young adults (15-40) and older adults (over 55). Non-Hodgkin lymphoma risk increases with age, typically affecting people over 60. 4. *Family history*: A family history of lymphoma may increase risk.
Treatment: Chemotherapy, radiation, targeted therapy, stem cell transplant
7. Pancreatic Cancer
Overview: Pancreatic cancer is a type of cancer that begins in the tissues of the pancreas, a gland located behind the stomach. The pancreas plays a critical role in digestion by producing enzymes that help break down food and hormones like insulin that regulate blood sugar levels. Pancreatic cancer is known for its aggressive nature and is often detected at a late stage, which makes it one of the deadliest forms of cancer.
Symptoms: 1. *Jaundice*: Yellowing of the skin and eyes due to bile duct obstruction caused by the tumor. 2. *Abdominal or back pain*: A common symptom, often occurring as the cancer grows and presses on surrounding organs or nerves. 3. *Unexplained weight loss*: Rapid and unintentional weight loss can be an early warning sign. 4. *Loss of appetite*. 5. *Nausea and vomiting*. 6. *New-onset diabetes*: Especially in people over 50 without a prior history of diabetes. 7. *Changes in stool*: Pale, greasy stools that float (steatorrhea) due to improper digestion of fats. 8. *Fatigue*. 9. *Blood clots*: Unexplained blood clots can sometimes be associated with pancreatic cancer.
Risk Factors:1. *Smoking*: Smoking significantly increases the risk, with smokers being two to three times more likely to develop pancreatic cancer. 2. *Age*: Most cases are diagnosed in people over 60, with the risk increasing with age. 3. *Family history*: A family history of pancreatic cancer or other cancers can increase risk due to inherited genetic mutations (e.g., BRCA1/BRCA2). 4. *Chronic pancreatitis*: Long-term inflammation of the pancreas, often caused by heavy alcohol use or genetic conditions, can raise the risk. 5. *Diabetes*: There is a strong link between long-standing diabetes and pancreatic cancer, although the exact relationship is not fully understood. 6. *Obesity*: Being overweight or obese is a risk factor.
Treatment: Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation.
8. Prostate Cancer
Overview: Occurs in the prostate, a gland in men that produces seminal fluid.
Symptoms:1. *Urinary symptoms*: - Frequent urination, especially at night. - Difficulty starting or stopping urination. - Painful urination or blood in the urine. 2. *Sexual symptoms*: - Difficulty achieving or maintaining an erection (erectile dysfunction). - Blood in the semen. 3. *Pain and discomfort*: - Pain in the lower back, hips, or pelvis may occur if the cancer has spread to the bones.
Risk Factors: 1. *Age*: The risk increases significantly after the age of 50, and most cases occur in men over 65. 2. *Race*: African-American men have a higher risk of developing prostate cancer and tend to have more aggressive forms of the disease. 3. *Family history: Men with a family history of prostate cancer (especially father or brother) are at higher risk. Inherited mutations like **BRCA1* and *BRCA2* also raise the risk. 4. *Genetics*: Certain genetic mutations, such as those in the BRCA genes, can increase the risk of prostate cancer. 5. *Diet*: A diet high in red meat and high-fat dairy products, along with low vegetable consumption, may slightly increase the risk. 6. *Obesity*: Obesity has been linked to more aggressive forms of prostate cancer.
Treatment: Surgery, radiation, hormonal therapy, chemotherapy.
9. Skin Cancer
Overview: cancer is the most common type of cancer, occurring when abnormal cells form in the skin’s outer layer due to damage, primarily from ultraviolet (UV) radiation. It can develop in any part of the body but most often appears on areas frequently exposed to the sun, like the face, neck, arms, and hands.
Symptoms: 1
  • Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)*:
    • - Pearly or waxy bumps, often with visible blood vessels. - Flat, flesh-colored, or brown scars-like lesions. - Bleeding or scabbing sores that don’t heal. 2. *Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)*: - Firm, red nodules. - Scaly, crusted growths that may bleed or develop into open sores. 3. *Melanoma*: - A new mole or changes in an existing mole, such as asymmetry, irregular borders, uneven color, or an increase in size. - Moles that itch, bleed, or become painful. - Brownish spots with darker speckles. - Dark lesions on the palms, soles, or under fingernails and toenails.
    Risk Factors: 1. *UV radiation exposure*: The primary cause of skin cancer, UV radiation from the sun or tanning beds, damages the DNA of skin cells, leading to abnormal growth. 2. *Fair skin*: People with fair skin, light-colored eyes, and red or blonde hair are at higher risk because they have less melanin, which offers some protection against UV rays. 3. *History of sunburns*: Repeated blistering sunburns, especially during childhood, increase the risk of skin cancer later in life. 4. *Moles*: Having many moles or atypical (dysplastic) moles increases the risk of melanoma.
    Treatment:Surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, targeted therapy.
    10. Uterine Cancer
    Overview: Forms in the uterus, commonly affecting the endometrial lining.It occurs when abnormal cells form in the lining of the uterus (endometrium). The majority of uterine cancers are endometrial cancers, although cancer can also develop in the muscles or other supporting tissues of the uterus, known as **uterine sarcoma*.
    Symptoms: 1. *Abnormal vaginal bleeding*: This is the most common symptom, especially after menopause. Any postmenopausal bleeding should be investigated. 2. *Unusual discharge*: Watery or blood-tinged discharge without the presence of blood can be a sign of early uterine cancer. 3. *Pelvic pain*: Pain or discomfort in the lower abdomen or pelvis, particularly in more advanced stages. 4. *Pain during intercourse*.
    Risk Factors:. *Age*: Most cases occur in postmenopausal women, typically in their 50s and 60s. 3. *Hormone replacement therapy (HRT)*: Women who take estrogen without progesterone after menopause have a higher risk of developing uterine cancer. 4. *Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)*: Women with PCOS have higher estrogen levels, which may increase their risk. 5. *Early menstruation or late menopause*: A longer exposure to estrogen due to early onset of menstruation (before age 12) or late menopause (after age 55) increases the risk. 6. *Family history: A family history of uterine cancer or hereditary conditions like **Lynch syndrome* increases the risk. 7. *Never having been pregnant*: Women who have not had children may have a higher risk of developing uterine cancer.
    Treatment: Surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, hormonal therapy
